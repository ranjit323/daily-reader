Remembering Big Bill

Thomas Jones · 22 Jul 2026

Occasionally when the phone rang at the London Review offices in the late 1990s it would be the operator asking if we’d accept a reverse charge call from Bill Johnson in Johannesburg. The first couple of times I checked with a colleague if it was OK to take it. After that I’d just say yes, put it on hold and call across the office to Mary-Kay Wilmers: ‘Big Bill’s on the phone.’ I never thought to ask where R.W. Johnson got his nickname; it certainly suited his voice. Once when I had to call him the conversation began as follows. TJ: ‘Hi Bill. It’s Tom at the LRB ...’ RWJ: ‘Ah! Tom O’LRB, like Lunchtime O’Booze.’

Yesterday the paper got an email from his son to say that Johnson had died at home in Cape Town. He was 82. For nearly 35 years he was one of the LRB’s more prolific contributors, writing 125 pieces between 1984 and 2017. Until 1995 he taught politics at Magdalen College, Oxford, where he had arrived as a Rhodes scholar from South Africa in 1964, and many of his pieces were unsparing looks at British and French politics (and politicians) of the 20th century. His best-known academic book was The Long March of the French Left, which was published in 1981. He first appeared in the paper on the Letters page, taking ‘mild issue’ with the review of that book. ‘Mild’ isn’t a word that one would associate with him.

He left Oxford to return to South Africa as director of the Helen Suzman Foundation, a position he held until 2001, and from where he turned his unsparing eye on the country’s post-liberation government. Reviewing South Africa’s Brave New World for the LRB in 2009, Robert Southall wrote:

Johnson’s South Africa is a country of bitter disappointment, of dashed hopes and dreams, brought about by the botched utopias of Mbeki and the ANC. His tale is compelling, yet in its simplicity and excess, it displays an alarming ahistoricism.

His stubborn unwillingness to examine his views or his language led to the LRB having to take down a piece he wrote for this blog during the 2010 World Cup after readers pointed out what we should already have seen for ourselves.

Discussing Johnson’s memoir Look Back in Laughter: Oxford’s Postwar Golden Age, Jeremy Harding wrote in the LRB in 2017 that

the ANC’s staunch affiliation with communism was Johnson’s core grudge against [it], and produces the flaw in his race sensibility: he could only account for the turn from non-racial politics to a politics of class struggle – with a lot of criminal activity under the rubric – by implying that the masses in urban South Africa were led astray by clever cosmopolitans with a scheming, internationalist agenda. During the 1950s and 1960s, race reactionaries in the US denounced white activism in the Civil Rights movement on similar grounds.

Johnson took umbrage at Harding’s ‘grossly insulting article’ and ‘resign[ed] as a writer for the LRB’. He had been a valuable contributor to the paper and Mary-Kay always admired him for his commitment to South Africa, whatever his criticisms of its leadership, and for returning to the country after the fall of apartheid.

One of his last pieces for the paper was an extraordinary account of contracting necrotising fasciitis after cutting his toes while swimming ‘in the idyllic Mpenjati lagoon’ in 2009:

I survived by a fluke; there’s no merit to it, though doctor friends try to make me feel good by telling me how strong I am and what a fight I put up. ‘Author Loses Leg in Lagoon’: my children saved the newspaper hoarding for me, its sheer banality a warning too. But mainly as I look at the waves I feel, ‘so far, so good.’ I spend no time at all regretting my left leg. It’s just so good to be alive.
