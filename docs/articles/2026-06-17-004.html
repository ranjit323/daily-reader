Loose Change

Hassan Ayman Herzallah · 17 Jun 2026

I arrived to take my exam after half the allotted time had already passed. I hadn’t been delayed by bombing, or even by a power cut, but by a worn-out banknote that no one was willing to accept.

I had woken up early, made a cup of mint tea and drunk it with my parents in the tent. It wasn’t yet eight o’clock and the exam didn’t start till nine. I had arranged to go with a friend to a place with electricity and internet – the exam was online – but he rang to tell me he had gone out to buy a few things for his family and would take the exam somewhere else. I would have to go alone.

I didn’t blame him, but his leaving without me had left me in some difficulty. He had cash, or at least was able to sort out a ride. I asked my parents if they had any cash. It was an unfair question. Where would they have got it from, when the commission for withdrawing cash was still as high as 25 per cent? I had some money in my digital wallet, but there was no time to find someone who could turn it into cash. By now it was 8.30 and walking to the internet café would take more than an hour.

I went to a few friends in the camp, asking for enough cash to get me to the cafeteria. One of them gave me a worn-out banknote and said it was all he had. He told me he had paid a 20 per cent commission to withdraw it, and suggested I try paying with my phone first. I smoothed the note with my fingers, as if pressing it flat could restore some of its value.

The first car I found was already full of people but had space for one more. I told the driver where I was going and asked if I could pay by app.

I took the note out of my pocket, handed it to him and opened the car door. But he looked at it, turned it over and gave it back to me as if it weren’t money. ‘Sorry my friend,’ he said. ‘This one is too worn out. If you don’t have another note or some change, you can’t get in.’

I didn’t know whether to close the car door or get in anyway. I could hear a clock ticking in my head. The note had a number printed on it. It was real money. But not if he wouldn’t accept it.

I stopped a car towing a trailer full of passengers and told the driver where I needed to go. ‘I’ll take you wherever you want,’ he said, ‘as long as you have cash.’ I told him I had only this note. He looked at it for a long moment, then said: ‘This won’t work.’

I asked the driver of a horse and cart if he could give me a lift and he told me to get in without asking for anything. After a while, he looked at me and said he knew me from the days when we were at home in Rafah. I smiled, but I didn’t recognise him.

The exam had already begun but I wasn’t thinking about the questions I was missing so much as what the man would say when I handed him the note. Eventually I gave it to him and told him it was all I had. He took it and gave me change. Before the war, small change had been a weight in my pocket, to be left in a drawer or forgotten at the bottom of a bag. Now it meant you could pay for a ride, buy an internet card, keep going.

Trying to get the cash to buy a kilo of flour, I had thought the problem was getting hold of cash itself: the commission, the waiting, the withdrawal, the loss before you have bought anything. But now I realised that it not only isn’t enough to have money in your bank account; sometimes it isn’t enough to have cash in your pocket. Small change can be more valuable than a large note.

At last we arrived at the café. I rushed in, bought an internet card and opened the exam page on my phone. There were only fourteen minutes left of the thirty-minute test. I did my best to answer what I could.
